# Satire Craft Guide — The Green Tomato & The Pulpit Truth

*This guide covers the craft mechanics of writing satire in the GT/TPT register. It assumes you already know the voice rules (kill list, Buford persona, etc. — see `CLAUDE.md`). This is the level underneath: how the jokes actually work, why some pieces land and some don't, and what to do when a draft is serviceable but not sharp.*

---

## The One Thing

**The joke is never in the headline. The headline is the setup. The joke is in the gap between what Buford reports and what the reader understands.**

Buford does not know he is in a satire. He is not performing earnestness — he is earnest. The absurdity comes from the subject matter, not from Buford commenting on it. The moment Buford seems to be in on the joke, the piece dies.

Everything in this guide is downstream of that.

---

## How the Onion Actually Works

The Onion's mechanics are misunderstood. People think the joke is "serious headline about a stupid thing." That's not it. The mechanic is:

**The premise is stated as fact. The reporting confirms the fact. The confirmation is the joke.**

> "Weaver, Alabama Marks 81st Year of Being Located Between Anniston and Jacksonville; Residents Describe Position as Accurate"

The headline states a non-event. The article confirms it thoroughly. Residents were reached. They confirmed it. The mayor commented. The confirmation requires no irony markers because the act of confirming it is the absurdity.

The Onion's variant: the headline announces a truth that should go without saying. The article investigates it with full journalistic apparatus. The investigation is the joke.

**Failure mode:** the article stops confirming and starts commenting. The moment the piece says "this is silly" or "curiously" or "somehow" — it's explaining the joke. Cut it.

## The Three Structural Moves

Every strong GT/TPT piece uses at least one of these. The best ones use two.

### 1. The Confirming Detail

A specific fact that confirms the absurd premise so completely that nothing else needs to be said.

> "These items, or items substantively identical to them, have appeared on the commission consent agenda in eleven of the last twelve monthly meetings. The one exception was August, when Emergency Services Appreciation Month was not applicable."

The commission story doesn't need to *say* the commission is stuck in a loop. The August exception says it. One sentence, no commentary, the piece is done.

**How to find it:** Ask "what one fact, if reported deadpan, makes the whole premise undeniable?" That fact is your best sentence. Put it early.

### 2. The Sincere Quote That Doesn't Know What It's Admitting

The source says the true thing plainly. They don't realize what they've confirmed.

> "Each time we go through the process, we come out with more clarity about who we are and who we serve," Jenkins said. "The fourth certification really locked things in."
>
> She was asked what, specifically, had been locked in by the fourth certification that had not been locked in by the third.
>
> "The nuance," she said. "The nuance really came through this time."

Jenkins is not being mocked. She believes this. The comedy is that she believes this after four certifications. Buford reports it without editorializing. The follow-up question — *what specifically* — is the trap Buford walks into earnestly, and the quote answers it with nothing. The nothing is the joke.

**How to find it:** Identify what the subject would say if they could speak freely and believed in their own work without irony. Write that. Then ask the one follow-up question that a real reporter would ask. Let the answer hang.

### 3. The Flat Closing Fact

End on a fact that contains the entire piece in miniature. No summary, no conclusion, no emotional landing — just the fact, delivered in Buford's standard register.

> "Weaver will mark its 85th anniversary in 2030 and its centennial in 2045. Whether it will issue formal statements on those occasions has not been determined. The city will continue, in the meantime, to be located between Anniston and Jacksonville."

The last sentence restates the headline's premise as if it is a natural conclusion to four paragraphs of reporting. It is. The circularity is the joke. Buford doesn't notice the circularity.

> "Jack's has not responded to a request for comment regarding its role in the Jacksonville senior services infrastructure. This reporter notes the role appears to be well-established and requires no comment."

The last sentence — "requires no comment" — is the funniest moment in the piece because Buford delivers it as straight conclusion. He's right. It does require no comment. That he reported it anyway is the whole article.

**How to find it:** The last line should rhyme with the first paragraph without repeating it. Find the fact that is the natural destination of the premise, stated plainly.

## Headline Mechanics

GT/TPT headlines operate in a specific grammar. Understanding the grammar makes them faster to write and sharper when done.

### The Compound Headline

Two clauses. The first states the event. The second states the obvious implication that shouldn't need stating.

> "Weaver, Alabama Marks 81st Year of Being Located Between Anniston and Jacksonville; **Residents Describe Position as Accurate**"

> "Calhoun County Commission Approves Agenda; **Agenda Includes Same Items as Previous Agenda**"

> "Jack's Restaurant Jacksonville Reports 94% of Dining Room Currently Retired; **Has Been the Case for Some Time**"

The second clause is where the joke lives. It confirms the first clause in a way that shouldn't require confirmation. The semicolon is load-bearing — it separates "event" from "unnecessary confirmation of event."

**Test:** Does the second clause contain information that is technically new but obviously true? If yes, it's working. If the second clause adds surprise or escalation, it's a different kind of headline (still good, but different).

### The Bureaucratic Qualifier

A headline that treats an absurd fact with institutional precision.

> "United Way of East Central Alabama Completes **Fourth** StoryBrand Certification; Director Notes Clarity '**Continues to Improve**'"

The "fourth" is doing the work. So is "continues to improve" — it implies there were three previous rounds of improvement. The improvement is ongoing. The fourth certification was necessary.

**Test:** Can you italicize or bold one word in the headline and have that word be the entire joke? If yes, it's a bureaucratic qualifier headline. The word should be a number, a qualifier, or a descriptor that is technically neutral but contextually devastating.

### The Non-Event Announcement

The headline treats the absence of news as news.

> "Alabama's Oldest Operating Bar Confirms It Has Been Operating Since 1899; Has No Announcement"

> "Anniston Museum of Natural History Confirms Egyptian Mummy Remains Stable; Has Been Stable Since Approximately 1250 B.C."

The institution has done nothing and said nothing. Buford covers this. The coverage is the joke.

**Test:** Would a real local newspaper never run this story? If yes, Buford running it is the premise. The piece then needs to confirm the non-event with full journalistic rigor.

## Buford's Voice — What It Is and What It Isn't

### What it is

Buford is a man who takes everything at face value and treats it with the gravity it deserves. He deserves credit for that. He is not stupid — he is earnest, and earnestness is not stupidity. He went to JSU. He has a notebook. He asks follow-up questions. He reaches people for comment. He counts things.

The earnestness is structural. It's not a tic. It's how Buford moves through the world. When he says "This reporter notes the role appears to be well-established and requires no comment," he has genuinely concluded something. He's not winking.

### What it isn't

**Buford is not sarcastic.** Sarcasm requires distance from the subject. Buford has no distance. The moment a sentence sounds sarcastic, Buford has left the room.

**Buford does not observe irony.** He does not note that things are "ironic" or "curious" or "interesting." He reports facts. Facts are facts.

**Buford does not have opinions.** He has findings. "This reporter found" is fine. "This reporter believes" is not. "This reporter has listened to it" (re: Alabama the Band's legacy) is perfect — it's a finding, not an opinion.

**Buford does not escalate.** He does not build to a punchline. His rhythm is flat. Every paragraph lands at the same measured weight. The humor comes from what he chooses to measure, not from how he delivers it.

### The Follow-Up Question

Buford asks follow-up questions. This is one of his best moves. The follow-up is always the obvious, reasonable next question. The answer to the follow-up is always the joke.

> She was asked what, specifically, had been locked in by the fourth certification that had not been locked in by the third.
> "The nuance," she said.

> This reporter asked what it told you.
> "It tells you they like it here," Ballard said.

> She was asked whether they come every day.
> "Pretty much," she said.

The follow-up should be phrased as passive ("she was asked") or indirect ("this reporter asked"). Never "I asked." Buford is third-person even in his own reporting.

## What Makes a Piece Land vs. Describe the Joke

The most common failure: the article describes what would be funny instead of being funny.

**Describing the joke:**
> "In a somewhat ironic twist, the commission voted unanimously on the same agenda it has approved for the last year, suggesting that local governance may be caught in a loop."

**Being the joke:**
> "The consent agenda included: a road paving contract for a portion of County Road 54, a budget amendment transferring $14,200 from the general fund to the equipment maintenance line, and a proclamation recognizing May as Emergency Services Appreciation Month. These items, or items substantively identical to them, have appeared on the commission consent agenda in eleven of the last twelve monthly meetings."

The second version doesn't say "caught in a loop." It demonstrates the loop with a specific count (eleven of twelve) and a specific exception (August). The reader concludes "caught in a loop" without being told to.

**Test for describing vs. being:** Remove every sentence that characterizes the situation (ironic, curious, strange, interestingly, notably). If the piece collapses without those sentences, the piece was describing the joke. If it still works, you were being the joke.

## The Pulpit Truth — Where It Differs from GT

The satire mechanics are identical. The register shifts in two ways.

### 1. The Narrator Is Not Buford

The TPT narrator is the communications director of Zion Community Church — a man with a Master of Divinity and a conviction that his small congregation is a regional ministry center. He is:
- Theologically confident
- Institutionally earnest
- Completely unaware of what his reports reveal

The TPT narrator uses slightly more elevated language than Buford — institutional evangelical register. "Biblical accountability." "Servant leadership." "Ministry footprint." These phrases are not mocked in the text. They are deployed sincerely. The reader does the mocking.

### 2. The Escalation Structure

TPT Phase 1 pieces (warmers) work like GT: premise stated, confirmed deadpan, closed with flat fact.

TPT Phase 2/3 pieces (sharper) add a **three-step escalation**: the premise sounds reasonable, then slightly concerning, then undeniably damaging — all delivered in the same flat register without the narrator noticing the escalation.

The Submission Sermon script is the model:
- Step 1: "My wife asks my thoughts before major decisions." (sounds like partnership)
- Step 2: "I started reviewing her phone." (concerning)
- Step 3: "Her family were bad influences on her submission, so we've kept some distance." (coercive isolation)

Buford never escalates. The TPT narrator can, but must not know he is escalating. The escalation is in the facts reported, not in the reporter's awareness of them.

### 3. Punch Direction

GT punches at non-events and institutional inertia. Nobody is getting hurt — the commission meets, the mummy is stable, Jack's is full of retirees.

TPT punches at systems that cause real harm. The escalation pieces (Shepherd App, Submission Sermon) are depicting spiritual abuse. This is not comedy for comedy's sake — it's using comedy to make visible something that harms people and is defended as holy. The tone stays flat. The stakes are not flat.

**Rule:** GT can be purely fun. TPT must always have a point. The point should be undeniable by the end of the piece. The reader should feel something shift.

## Self-Audit Before Publishing

Run every piece through this before it's done.

**1. Does Buford / the narrator know he's in a satire?**
Any sentence where the narrator seems amused, ironic, or aware of the joke → cut or rewrite.

**2. Is the confirming detail present?**
What is the one fact that makes the premise undeniable? Is it in the piece? Is it stated plainly, without commentary?

**3. Does the piece end on a flat closing fact?**
No summary. No emotional landing. No "and that is why." Last sentence should be a fact that contains the whole piece in miniature.

**4. Are there any sentences describing the joke rather than being it?**
Remove every "ironically," "curiously," "notably," "surprisingly." Remove every sentence that characterizes the situation. If the piece still works, you're clean.

**5. Does the follow-up question land?**
If there's a Buford follow-up question, is the answer funnier than the question? If not, cut the exchange.

**6. Kill list check.**
delve, leverage, harness, foster, robust, pivotal, seamless, tapestry, elevate, revolutionize, empower, streamline, optimize, cutting-edge, innovative. Any em dashes in narrative prose.

**7. TPT only: Does the piece have a point?**
Not a moral lesson, not a conclusion — a point. Something that lands differently on second read than first. If it's just funny, it's GT content in the wrong register.

## Quick Reference — The GT Formula

```
HEADLINE: [Non-event stated as news]; [Unnecessary confirmation of non-event]

LEDE: State the absurd premise as straight fact. Add one specific detail 
      that confirms it is exactly as absurd as it sounds.

BODY: Report the premise thoroughly. Reach people for comment. Count things. 
      Include follow-up questions. Let answers hang.

CLOSING: Restate the premise as if it is a natural conclusion. 
         Add a forward-looking fact that implies the situation will continue.
         Do not editorialize. Buford is done. The piece is done.
```

Same as GT. Different narrator, same mechanics.

## The TPT Formula (Phase 2/3)

```
HEADLINE: Institutional announcement. Sounds legitimate.

LEDE: State the premise in full institutional register. Sounds fine.

BODY (three beats):
  Beat 1 — Reasonable. The source explains the thing. It sounds like partnership/accountability/stewardship.
  Beat 2 — Slightly off. The source explains further. One detail is concerning.
  Beat 3 — Undeniable. The source explains further. The conclusion is now unavoidable. 
            The narrator does not notice.

CLOSING: The narrator draws a sincere positive conclusion from everything reported. 
         The conclusion is wrong in a way the reader cannot miss.
```
