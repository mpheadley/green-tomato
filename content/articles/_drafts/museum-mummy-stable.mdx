ANNISTON, Ala. — The Anniston Museum of Natural History has confirmed that its Egyptian mummy, which dates to approximately 1250 B.C., remains in stable condition. The mummy has been stable since approximately 1250 B.C. Museum staff do not anticipate a change.

The mummy, displayed in the museum's Egyptian antiquities exhibit, is approximately 3,276 years old. It arrived at the museum in the mid-twentieth century and has not moved under its own power. A museum spokesperson confirmed this.

"The mummy is fine," said Dr. Patricia Weems, the museum's curator of natural and human history. "It has always been fine, in the sense that it has not changed. It is what it is, and what it is has been consistent."

This reporter asked if there had been any recent developments regarding the mummy.

"No," she said.

The museum's Egyptian exhibit also includes funerary objects, scarab amulets, and a collection of artifacts consistent with burial practices of the New Kingdom period. These items are also stable. Dr. Weems confirmed they have not been updated.

The Anniston Museum of Natural History houses exhibits spanning seven continents of natural and cultural history. All seven continents are represented. Anniston is on none of them. The museum considers this to be the nature of collections.

The mummy is available for viewing Tuesday through Saturday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., and Sunday from 1 to 5 p.m. Admission is $8 for adults. The mummy does not have its own hours. It is always there.
