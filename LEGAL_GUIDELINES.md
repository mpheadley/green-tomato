# The Green Tomato: Legal & Parody Guidelines

This document outlines the legal standards and creative guidelines for using real-world names, brands, and likenesses in articles and images for The Green Tomato. The goal is to maximize comedic effect while minimizing legal risk.

## Guiding Principle: Parody & Fair Use

The Green Tomato operates as a work of satire. This classification provides strong legal protection under the principles of Fair Use and the First Amendment, as long as a "reasonable person" would understand that the content is a joke and not a factual claim. The site-wide satire disclaimer is our first line of defense and statement of intent.

---

## 1. Use of Names in Text (Articles)

**Policy: Permitted and Encouraged.**

It is legally sound and creatively essential to use the real names of businesses (e.g., Chick-fil-A, Olive Garden), places (e.g., Quintard Mall, Noble Street), and public figures in the text of articles.

### Legal Justification:

*   **Nominative Fair Use:** We are using the name to identify the subject of our commentary. This is legally protected.
*   **Parody:** The humor comes from placing these real entities into absurd, obviously fictional situations. This is a protected form of speech.

### Safety Rule:

*   **Avoid Defamation:** The fictional situation must be clearly absurd. We must never make a *believable but false* factual claim that could harm the reputation of a person or business (e.g., "The Olive Garden failed a health inspection"). Our stories must always be identifiable as jokes.

---

## 1a. Real People — Hard Rule: Never Use Real Individual Names as Characters

**Policy: Never name real living individuals as sources, spokespeople, or characters in articles.**

Real institutions (Knox Foundation, Alabama Ballet, City of Anniston) may be named — they are subjects of satire, not characters. But individual people who work for or run those institutions must always be replaced with fictional names.

**Why:** A fictional organization doing something absurd is clearly satire. A named real person saying fictional things is potentially defamatory — the reader might not know the quote is invented.

## 2. Use of Logos & Branding in Images

**Policy: Parody, Do Not Replicate.**

It is our policy **never to use official, trademarked logos or branding** of real companies in our generated images. Direct replication carries a risk of trademark infringement claims.

Instead, we will create **transformative parodies** of these visuals. This is a common and legally defensible technique used in satire.

### Approved Techniques for Parody Images:

1.  **Alteration / Misspelling:** Change the name to something phonetically or visually similar (e.g., "McDowell's" instead of "McDonald's," "Burger Queen" instead of "Burger King").
2.  **Partial or Obscured Logos:** Show only a small, generic piece of a logo, with the rest cut off or obscured. For example, a red circle with a small piece of a white swirl for "Coca-Cola."
3.  **Style Imitation:** Mimic the color scheme, font style, and overall aesthetic of a brand without using its name or logo directly. This evokes the brand in the viewer's mind without infringing on it.

This approach allows us to maintain a strong visual connection to our satirical targets in a way that is creatively interesting and legally sound.
